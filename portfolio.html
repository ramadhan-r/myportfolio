Land Use/Land Cover Mapping and Change Detection

Mapping and analysis of land cover patterns using satellite imagery to assess vegetation distribution and changes over time.

The side-by-side vegetation cover maps for 2017 and 2025 show a broadly similar spatial distribution of dense, sparse, and non-vegetated areas across Nyeri County. Dense vegetation remains concentrated in high-altitude and protected forest zones, while sparse vegetation continues to dominate agricultural and transitional areas, and non-vegetated land is largely confined to urban centres and major infrastructure corridors. Although slight visual differences can be observed between the two maps, these alone are insufficient to accurately quantify gains or losses in vegetation cover. Meaningful interpretation of increases, decreases, or transitions in vegetation therefore requires a dedicated change detection map, which explicitly identifies areas of stability, degradation, and regeneration between the two time periods.

The post-classification change detection analysis revealed that Nyeri County experienced both vegetation gains and losses between 2017 and 2025. Stable vegetation dominated the landscape, with dense-to-dense transitions covering approximately 1,920 km², indicating minimal disturbance in protected and high-altitude forest areas. However, notable degradation was observed where dense vegetation transitioned to sparse vegetation (175 km²), particularly in agricultural and peri-urban zones. Vegetation regeneration was also evident, with sparse vegetation converting to dense vegetation over 272 km², suggesting successful natural regrowth and conservation efforts in some areas.

Urban Heat Island Analysis

Urban Heat Island (UHI) refers to the phenomenon where urban areas experience higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas due to human activities and built environments. This analysis helps inform urban planning and mitigation strategies.

The Land Surface Temperature (LST) map of Kisumu City reveals distinct temperature variations across different urban zones. The central business district and densely built-up areas exhibit higher temperatures, often exceeding 35°C, due to extensive concrete surfaces and limited vegetation. In contrast, peripheral areas with more green spaces and water bodies show lower temperatures, typically below 30°C. This spatial distribution highlights the impact of urbanization on local climate, emphasizing the need for green infrastructure and urban planning strategies to mitigate UHI effects.
